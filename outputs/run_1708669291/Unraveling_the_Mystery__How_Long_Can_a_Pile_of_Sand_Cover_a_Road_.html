Unraveling the Mystery: How Long Can a Pile of Sand Cover a Road?

23/02/2024

Imagine you have a conical pile of sand and a mission: to cover a 4m wide road with a layer of sand 10cm thick. The first hurdle in this seemingly simple task is understanding the dimensions you're working with. The sand pile's shape is a cone, with a base circumference of 31.4m and a height of 1.2m. The question is, how long can this pile of sand cover the road?

To embark on this mathematical journey, the initial step is to find the radius of the cone's base. The radius is a crucial piece of information because it is instrumental in calculating the volume of the cone, which, in turn, will help us determine the amount of sand available. The circumference of a circle (or the base of our cone, in this case) is given by the formula $C = 2\pi r$. Rearranging this formula to solve for $r$ gives us the radius as $r = \frac{31.4}{2\pi}$, which simplifies to $r = 5$ meters.

Now that we know the radius, we can calculate the volume of the cone. Why do we need the volume? It's simple: knowing the volume allows us to determine how much space the sand can fill. The volume of a cone is calculated using the formula $V = \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h$. Plugging in our values, we get $V_{\text{cone}} = \frac{1}{3} \pi \times 5^2 \times 1.2$, which approximately equals $31.4$ cubic meters. This calculation reveals the total volume of sand at our disposal.

The final step in our quest is to find out how long the sand can cover the road, given the road's width and the desired thickness of the sand layer. To solve this, we apply the formula for the volume of a rectangular prism (or in our scenario, the layer of sand on the road), $V = lwh$. Rearranging to solve for the length ($l$), we get $l = \frac{V_{\text{cone}}}{4 \times 0.1}$. With $V_{\text{cone}}$ being $31.4$ cubic meters, the length of the road that can be covered by the sand is approximately $78.5$ meters.

This mathematical journey not only showcases the beauty of applying geometry and algebra to solve real-world problems but also provides a clear answer to our initial question. With a conical pile of sand described, one can cover a 4m wide road with a 10cm thick layer of sand for about 78.5 meters. This solution not only satisfies our curiosity but also demonstrates the practical application of mathematical principles in everyday scenarios.
