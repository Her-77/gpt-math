Calculating Speed in a Meeting Point Problem

23/02/2024

Understanding how to solve problems involving distances, speeds, and time can often be quite challenging. However, with the right approach, these problems can become manageable and even enjoyable. In this article, we will explore a problem where two vehicles start from point A to point B, 120 km apart, and meet at point C after 2 hours, before turning back towards their starting points. Our goal is to find the speed of the passenger car.

To begin with, it's important to establish the combined speed of both vehicles. This initial step is crucial because it allows us to relate the speeds of the two vehicles, given that they cover a fixed distance in a specific time frame. By understanding that the two vehicles meet after 2 hours, we deduce that their combined speed is 60 km/h. This is because they cover the distance between A and C (which is half the total distance) in 2 hours, indicating that $x + y = 60$ km/h, where $x$ is the speed of the passenger car and $y$ is the speed of the other vehicle.

Next, we set up a relationship between the distances traveled by both vehicles. This step is based on the observation that they meet at the same place twice, suggesting a consistent ratio between their speeds. Given that the distances covered by the vehicles in relation to their speeds and the times spent allow us to set up an equation based on their meeting points, we conclude that the distance to the meeting point and back is the same for both vehicles when they meet the second time. Thus, we establish that $2d = 2d$, where $d$ represents the distance each vehicle covers to the meeting point and back.

The final step involves solving the equations for the speed of the passenger car. By applying system of linear equations solving techniques, such as substitution or elimination, we can find the exact speed of the passenger car. When we solve the equations, taking into account the constant total speed and the distance relationship, we find that the speed of the passenger car, $x$, is 40 km/h, and the speed of the other vehicle, $y$, is 20 km/h.

This problem not only tests our ability to manipulate and solve equations but also challenges us to think critically about the relationships between speed, time, and distance. By breaking down the problem into manageable steps and systematically solving the equations, we can uncover the solution and gain deeper insights into how these concepts interact in real-world scenarios.
