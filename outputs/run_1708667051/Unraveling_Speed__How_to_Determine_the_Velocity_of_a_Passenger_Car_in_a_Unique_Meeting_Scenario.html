Unraveling Speed: How to Determine the Velocity of a Passenger Car in a Unique Meeting Scenario

23/02/2024

Have you ever wondered how to figure out the speed of vehicles based on their meeting points? Today, we'll delve into a fascinating math problem that involves a passenger car, a truck, and their intriguing encounters on a journey between two towns 120 km apart. The key to solving this riddle lies in understanding the concept of relative speed and employing a bit of algebra.

To set the stage, the problem presents us with a scenario where a passenger car and a truck meet at the same point twice while traveling between these two towns. The first meeting occurs after 2 hours. This pivotal piece of information allows us to use the concept of relative speed. Relative speed is essentially the rate at which the distance between two moving objects changes. When objects move towards each other, as in our case, their relative speed is the sum of their individual speeds. Applying this knowledge, we establish our first equation: $2(V_p + V_t) = 120$, where $V_p$ and $V_t$ represent the speeds of the passenger car and the truck, respectively.

The plot thickens with the realization that for the car and truck to meet at the same point twice, the passenger car must have made a round trip. This insight is crucial as it points towards the symmetry of the problem and allows us to further refine our understanding of the distances covered. By considering the round trip for the passenger car and a single trip for the truck to the meeting point, we deduce that the speed of the passenger car is twice that of the truck, mathematically expressed as $V_p = 2V_t$. This equation is a direct consequence of the relationship between speed, distance, and time.

With two equations at our disposal, solving the system reveals the speeds of both the passenger car and the truck. Through either substitution or elimination methods, we find that $V_p = 60$ km/h and $V_t = 30$ km/h. This solution not only satisfies our original equations but also beautifully ties back to the given scenario, confirming that the math indeed checks out.
