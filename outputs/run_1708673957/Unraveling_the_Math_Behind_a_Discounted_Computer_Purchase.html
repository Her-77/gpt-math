Unraveling the Math Behind a Discounted Computer Purchase

23/02/2024

When it comes to shopping, who doesn't love a good discount? Imagine you're eyeing a sleek computer priced at 8000 yuan, but the thought of shelling out that much could make anyone hesitant. This is where a tempting 20% discount comes into play, promising a more palatable price. But what does a 20% discount really mean, and how do we calculate the final price after such a discount?

The concept of a '20% discount' essentially means that the buyer gets to pay 20% less than the original price. In mathematical terms, this translates to paying 80% (or 0.8 times) of the original price. So, if our computer is tagged at 8000 yuan, applying a 20% discount is as simple as calculating 80% of 8000 yuan. The mathematics behind this is straightforward: $0.8 \times 8000 = 6400$ yuan. Therefore, the price Dad hopes to pay for the computer, after the discount, is 6400 yuan.

However, the story takes a slight twist when the manager proposes to add an additional 50 yuan to the discounted price. This counteroffer might seem minor, but it changes the final amount Dad has to pay. To find the actual price after this adjustment, we need to add 50 yuan to the already calculated discounted price of 6400 yuan.

Adding the 50 yuan is a simple arithmetic operation. The calculation would look something like this: $6400 + 50 = 6450$ yuan. Thus, the final price that Dad ends up paying for the computer, after the discount and the additional charge, comes to 6450 yuan. This is a classic example of how understanding and applying basic mathematical operations can help make sense of real-world scenarios, such as calculating discounts and final prices.

Understanding discounts and final prices is not only crucial for making informed purchasing decisions but also serves as a practical application of mathematics in our daily lives. Through this example, we see how a combination of percentage calculation and simple addition helps us navigate through discount offers and counteroffers, ultimately leading us to the actual amount that needs to be paid.
